DEFINITION

Defining definition

    Definition is a clear explanation of what a word or phrase means. It helps people understand the specific idea or object being described. Definitions often include important details about the characteristics or features of the word, making it easier for someone to grasp its meaning.

The 4 different kinds of definition that are useful in technical writing

  • INFORMAL DEFINITION
  • FORMAL DEFINITION
  • EXPANDED DEFINITION
  • STIPULATIVE DEFINITION

INFORMAL DEFINITION

    An informal definition is a short explanation of a word that might be unfamiliar to the reader. It usually gives a synonym or a simple phrase to help clarify the meaning.

    An informal definition is a short explanation of a word that might be unfamiliar to the reader. It usually gives a synonym or a simple phrase to help clarify the meaning. These definitions are placed in parentheses or separated by commas or dashes right after the word, making it easy for readers to understand quickly.

Example of  Informal  Definition

    An agenda (a list of items to be discussed in a meeting) helps keep the meeting organized and on track.

Explanation: Here, the term "agenda" might not be well understood by all readers. The informal definition offers a quick explanation in parentheses, showing that it’s a tool used to structure meetings, enhancing understanding of its importance in office settings.

FORMAL DEFINITION

    A formal definition is more precise, complete, and logically structured than an informal one. Whereas an informal definition can be given in a word or phrase, a formal definition is always constructed as a sentence with three separate and necessary parts. The first part, called the species, is the term you will define. The second part is the genus, or the class in which that term belongs. The third part consists of the differentiae, or the distinguishing features that separate the term from all other members of the same class. Think of a formal definition as an equation in sentence form.

TIPS OF WRITING FORMAL DEFINITION

  1. Avoid repeating key words. Repeating in the differentiae the word you want to define gives readers a circular definition that leads nowhere.
  2. Avoid using language that is too technical for your reader. Defining one technical word with another may baffle even an audience of experts
  3. Be careful the differentiae are not too broad. Your distinguishing features must be sufficiently narrow so they cannot apply to any other object or process.
  4. Be careful the differentiae are not too narrow. When your differentiae are too narrow, you may exclude members of the class you want to define.
  5. Never start a definition with "ls where," "ls when," or "is a kind of." Beginning with "is where" or "is when" violates the formal logic of the definition.

TEN WAYS TO EXPAND DEFINITION

  • ETYMOLOGY - is the study of the origin and history of words, including how their meanings and structures have evolved over time. It often traces a word back to its roots in ancient languages like Greek, Latin, or even older sources. Etymology helps us understand the original meanings of words, as well as how they've changed to become part of modern language.

 • ORIGIN & APPLICATION - refers to the background information about an object or process, encompassing four key aspects: (1) the discovery, which includes who invented or discovered it and when; (2) its early uses and any significant challenges or problems encountered during that time; (3) the changes made in its construction, design, or application over time; and (4) the impact of these changes on its current usage and potential future developments. This comprehensive understanding helps clarify the evolution and significance of the object or process in question.

• EXCLUSION - is a way to define something by explaining what it is not. Instead of focusing on what the item is or its features, you clarify its meaning by pointing out what doesn’t belong to it. This method helps people understand the item better by comparing it to other related things.

These might include materials like sandpaper, steel wool, and polishing compounds. By providing specific examples, it becomes easier for people to understand what abrasive materials are and how they are used.

• COMPARISON & CONTRAST - Comparisons establish likenesses; contrasts emphasize differences. Used together or separately, these techniques assist you in clarifying a term for the reader. • CAUSE & EFFECT - This technique tells the reader why something happens and what follows from its occurrence. In defining lightning, magnetism, a policy, or a disease, you might begin with the causes and then describe the effects. • METHOD OF OPERATION - An explanation of how something works, is made, or occurs is essential for understanding various processes, whether they are procedures, manufacturing methods, construction techniques, or natural phenomena. • DESCRIPTION - is a way of explaining what something is like by detailing its features and characteristics. This method uses sensory details such as sight, sound, smell, taste, and touch to help the reader or listener visualize the subject better and understand it more clearly.

• CLASSIFICATION - is a method of organizing or explaining a complex term or concept by dividing it into smaller, more manageable parts or categories. This approach allows for a clearer understanding by examining each unit individually, making it easier to grasp the overall idea.

STIPULATIVE  DEFINITION

    Is a type of definition that assigns a specific meaning to a word or phrase for the purposes of a particular discussion or argument.

Explanation: A stipulative definition is a type of definition that specifies how a word or phrase will be used in a particular context, rather than providing its common or traditional meaning. This kind of definition is useful in discussions, debates, or academic arguments where precise language is crucial for clarity and understanding. By establishing a specific meaning for a term, it helps to avoid confusion and ensures that all participants in the conversation are on the same page regarding what is being discussed. For example, if a researcher defines "sustainability" in a certain way for their study, that definition applies only to that context, and others should refer to it to understand the research accurately.

CLASSIFICATION AND PARTITION

CLASSIFICATION

Defining Classification

   Classification is a process where you group or organize things based on common characteristics to make it easier for us to understand.

      In writing, It is like sorting things into groups based on what they have in common just like you might organize your notes and pens or anything inside your bag that you use for school. Classification helps readers understand better because you're explaining one group at a time

Establishing the Basis for Classification

     Establishing a basis for classification means choosing a clear reason or rule for how you will group things. It’s like deciding what you’ll use to sort things before you start sorting.

How to Use Classification

1. Select one basis of classification

  • When you select one basis, you should decide what you’ll focus on to organize everything. For instance, if you choose size, you’ll group things only based on their size.

2.  Use a principle of classification that is meaningful to your audience and purpose.

  •  You should choose a way of organizing things that makes sense to the people you're talking to and fits your goal.

3. Maintain consistency among categories

  • Maintaining consistency among categories, you create a clear and organized structure that helps your audience easily understand and navigate the information you present.

4. Make sure categories do not overlap

  •  Ensuring that categories do not overlap means making sure that each item or element fits into only one category.

5. Be inclusive

  • This means that when we organize or sort things, we include everyone and everything that should be in those groups. You have to make sure that all essential categories are included.

6. Present your categories in a logical order

  • Presenting your categories in logical order means arranging them in a way that makes sense and is easy for others to understand. This helps people find what they’re looking for without getting confused.

PARTITION

Defining Partition

    It is also a basic technique of analysis like classification but partition operates entirely differently. Where partition divides the component into sections or parts for clarity and organization. The parts do not necessarily have anything in common beyond the fact that they belong to the same unit.

Establishing the Basis for Partition

    An object can be partitioned in many ways, depending on the principle you use, which depends on your audience's needs.

How to Use Partition

1. Select a single object to partition

  • Be careful of selecting to avoid selecting a term that looks singular but is plural such as crime, energy, or soil. These would be impossible to break down as these terms include numerous and complex classes.

2. Follow a systematic principle in dividing

  • Divide the components according to a systematic principle that is consistent with the audience and your purpose.

3. Avoid Overlapping

  • Make sure that components do not overlap. One of the most misleading things for readers is to see a part listed in two different places.

4. Include all the components

  • Make sure that you include all components. If you omit a part, readers will not have a clear and complete picture of the object. If a part is optional or found on only a certain model or piece of equipment, tell readers so.

5. Follow a logical order of presentation.

  • Arrange your discussion in chronological order. This makes your presentation clear and organized.

PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION OF MECHANISM

What is Physical Description?

    Physical description - refers to a detailed account of the observable characteristics of an object, mechanism, or system. It focuses on aspects such as size, shape, color, material, and function, allowing the audience to visualize and understand the subject's form and how it operates.

Purpose of Physical Description

    The purpose of a physical description - is to communicate a clear and detailed understanding of an object, mechanism, or system to an audience.

Basic Elements of Content and Style

  • Be Comprehensive:

Provide an in-depth description that covers all relevant aspects of the mechanism. This includes its dimensions, materials used, design features, and operational elements.

  • Determine Your Audience Needs:

Tailor your description to the knowledge level and interests of your audience. For technical audiences, use precise terminology; for general audiences, simplify complex concepts.

  • Be Objective:

Maintain neutrality by presenting facts without personal opinions or biases. This enhances credibility and allows the audience to form their own conclusions.

  • Use Graphic Language:

Incorporate vivid and precise language to create a clear mental image. Use metaphors and similes where appropriate to enhance understanding without overwhelming the reader.

ORGANIZATIONS

    Effective organization of descriptions is crucial for clarity and reader engagement. Descriptions can be organized from various perspectives such as top to bottom, front to back, right to left, outside to inside, or according to specific criteria like size, growing cycles, mating and spawning seasons, distribution, or economic uses. For mechanical descriptions, it is common to identify major parts first and then examine each in detail.

    Before writing a description, it is essential to carefully determine (1) the functional or structural elements of the object and (2) the sequence of presenting these elements that will best meet the reader's needs. For instance, describing a car should involve grouping related parts together and presenting them in a logical order that aids understanding. The organization of a description should be tailored to the audience's needs. For example, a description of a crash cart for nurses should start at the top where the cardiac monitor is located and move to the drawers in the order they are accessed by the rescue team. Similarly, a description of a desk calculator for the operator might begin with the inside and move to the outside, focusing on the internal assembly process, while for consumers, it might start from the outside and emphasize the calculator's attractiveness and ease of use.

VISUALS

  • is a crucial role in enhancing the clarity and effectiveness of descriptions. They can convey information more immediately and accurately than words alone. For instance,

Photographs - provide a realistic portrayal of objects, making them invaluable for audiences unfamiliar with the subject matter.

Line drawings - are particularly useful as they can present an object from multiple perspectives and highlight specific details through callouts.

Cutaway drawings - offer insights into the internal components of an object by removing its external covering.

Exploded drawings - illustrate how an object is constructed by showing its individual parts and their interconnections.

Maps - on the other hand, are essential for conveying information about large areas or specific details such as topography, population, or roads.

Tables - are effective for arranging numerical data in a structured format, often used in specifications or detailed lists.

Graphs - represent relationships among different sets of numbers through curves or lines, making complex data more understandable.

Flow Charts - use block diagrams, pictures, and line drawings connected by arrows to depict the sequence of events or stages in a process.

Schematics - are diagrams that illustrate electrical or mechanical systems or interior configurations, providing detailed insights into complex structures.

Overall, the strategic use of these various types of visual photographs, line drawings, cutaway drawings, exploded drawings, maps, tables, graphs, charts, and schematics ensures that descriptions are clear, useful, and engaging for diverse audiences.

Guidelines for Using Visuals Clearly and Effectively:

1. Clarity and Simplicity:

  • Visuals should be clear and complete, but avoid unnecessary detail. Focus on the essential elements.

Example: Instead of a complex diagram of a printer showing every wire and component, use a simplified version highlighting only the main parts like the paper tray, ink cartridges, and print head.

 

2. Accurate Labeling:

  • Label important parts of visuals accurately, especially in diagrams like exploded views or cutaways. Labels should be horizontal whenever possible.

Example:  In a diagram of a printer, label each part clearly and ensure the labels are easy to read. For example, "Paper Tray," "Ink Cartridges," "Print Head."

 

3. Consistent Units and Terminology:

  • Use consistent units and terminology throughout the text and visuals. Avoid mixing metric and imperial units.

Example: If a diagram uses centimeters to measure the paper size, ensure the text also uses centimeters, not inches.

 

4. Referencing Visuals:

  • All visuals should be mentioned and referred to in the text.

Example: As shown in Figure 1, the printer has a paper tray that holds up to 100 sheets.

5. Placement of Visuals:

  • Place visuals close to their reference in the text, ideally after the reference, not before.

Example: A diagram of a printer's internal components should appear after the text describing the components.

 

6. Titles and Numbering:

  • Visuals should have titles (captions or cutlines). If multiple visuals are used, number them consecutively.

Example: Figure 1: Printer Paper Tray

 

7. Scale and Familiarity:

  • If a visual is a scale model, provide a scale. If the reader might not be familiar with the object, indicate if it's life-size, enlarged, or reduced.

Example: Figure 2: Scale model of a printer (1:10 scale)

8. Source Acknowledgment:

  • If a visual comes from another source, acknowledge the source after the caption and visual number.

Example: Figure 3: Adapted from [Source Name]

DESCRIPTION OF A PROCESS

2 Types of Process Writing

  • Process Instruction: This process requires the readers to perform steps
  • Process Explanation: Describes the stages or phenomena without action from the reader.

What is Process Explanation?

  • Process  explanation is a way of helping someone understand how something is done or how something works. You don't need the reader or the person to actually do the process, but you want them to understand it step by step. It's like telling a story about how something happens.
  • Unlike process instructions, where the goal is for the reader to follow steps and perform actions, a process explanation is purely informative. It guides the reader through a sequence of events or stages, helping them comprehend what happens during each part of the process without requiring them to take any action.

Purpose of Process Explanation

  • To help the reader understand the process without having to perform the steps. It provides insight into what happens during each stage of the process.
  • The primary goal of process explanation is to help the reader understand how something functions or develops over time. It’s not about performing tasks but providing a detailed walkthrough of how an event or a mechanism unfolds, ensuring the reader has a clear understanding of what happens at each stage.

Content of Process Explanation

  1. How Something is Made or Done
  • This type of process explanation tells the reader about the steps involved in creating or completing something. It’s like explaining how a product is manufactured or how a task is completed. The focus is on showing the steps involved in making or doing something, from start to finish.
  1. How Something Works
  • This content explains the function or operation of a system or machine. It describes what happens inside a device, process, or system to make it work. This is useful when explaining how technology or machinery works, or how a natural process takes place.
  1. Why or How Something Happens
  • This content explains the reason behind something happening or the mechanism that makes it occur. It describes causes and effects. This type of explanation is useful when explaining natural events, scientific phenomena, or social processes.
  1. Are There People or Machines Involved?
  • This part explains who or what is involved in the process. Some processes are done by people, while others are handled by machines or automated systems. It helps readers understand who or what performs each step in the process.

Structure of Process Explanation

  1. Introduction
  • The introduction sets the stage for your explanation. It should:
  • State what the process is: Clearly identify the topic.
  • Explain why you’re explaining it: Provide context to engage the reader.
  • In process explanation writing, the introduction is where you set the stage for the reader. It should identify what the process is and explain why it matters. The goal here is to provide context, so the reader understands why understanding the process is important. In a phone call example, the introduction would explain why knowing how to answer a phone call properly is crucial for professional communication in the workplace.

For example, you might say, “This explanation will guide you through the process of answering a phone call using Telephone in an office setting. Whether you are new to a workplace or simply looking to improve your telephone etiquette, understanding these steps can help create positive impressions and maintain professionalism.”

  1. Body
  • The body is the core of your explanation, where you:
  • The body is where the step-by-step explanation of the process takes place. Each step should be presented in chronological order (an arrangement of events in the order), detailing what happens in each stage of the process.
  1. Conclusion
  • Summary of the process: The conclusion summarizes the main points and reinforces the simplicity of the steps involved.
  • It is a brief summary of the whole process. It highlights why the process is important by pointing out the main steps and showing how effective or useful it is to follow them.

EXAMPLE OF PROCESS INSTRUCTION AND EXPLANATION

HOW TO ANSWER CALL USING AN OFFICE TELEPHONE?

PROCESS INSTRUCTION

  1. Pick up the telephone or press the answer button when the phone rings.
  2. Greet the caller
  3. Listen carefully to the caller’s request or concern.
  4. Look up information if the caller needs it or ask someone for help.
  5. Transfer the call if necessary, and tell the caller who they will speak with next.
  6. Summarize the conversation before ending the call.
  7. Thank the caller and say goodbye politely.

PROCESS EXPLANATION

As an office administration student and future office professional, mastering the skill of answering a phone call is essential. This process is vital for effective communication in the workplace, enabling interactions with clients and colleagues. Knowing how to properly answer a phone call not only enhances communication but also helps in creating a positive impression of the organization.

The process of answering a phone call begins when the office telephone rings. The first step is to lift the telephone from the base or press the answer button.

After answering, it’s crucial to greet the caller warmly. A suitable greeting might be, “Good morning, this is (Caller’s Name). How may I assist you today?” This introduction sets a professional tone for the conversation.

Next, it’s important to listen actively to the caller's request or concern. This means paying close attention to what the caller is saying and how they are saying it. If the caller is seeking specific information, be prepared to look it up or consult with a colleague to provide accurate answers. If the call needs to be transferred, inform the caller courteously about the transfer and explain who they will be speaking with next.

As the conversation ends, summarize the main points discussed, thank the caller for their time, and end the call on a polite note, such as, “Thank you for calling, and have a wonderful day!”

By following these steps, you, as an office administration student and future office professional, can effectively manage phone calls, leading to improved communication and more professional interactions. Mastering this process is crucial for building strong relationships in any business environment.

  • The example highlights the difference between process instructions and process explanation in the context of answering a phone call using an office telephone. Process instructions provide simple, step-by-step directions that are easy to follow. However, process explanation gives a deeper understanding of the significance of these steps within a professional setting, emphasizing how effective communication enhances client interactions and contributes to a positive image for the organization.

Importance of Process Explanation in Profession

  • Process explanations are a key part of many jobs. Engineers often need to describe the mechanical or electrical steps involved in construction projects. Health care workers also use these explanations, whether it’s about how a disease progresses, how medical equipment works, or the steps involved in a surgical procedure. When someone writes a proposal, they need to explain how the work will be done and how it will be tracked. Salespeople might explain how their company makes products. Additionally, investigative reports often include process explanations to detail how researchers collected and analyzed data.

Guidelines for writing

1. Follow the Right Order

  • Make sure to list the steps in the correct order. This helps the reader follow along without getting confused.

2. Include Every Step

  • Make sure to cover every step in the process. Skipping a step can lead to misunderstandings. If there are optional steps, make sure to mention those too.

3. Use Numbers and Headings

  • Organize your writing with numbers, headings, and subheadings. This helps readers see where one step ends and another begins, making it easier to follow.

4. Explain Each Step Clearly

  • Describe what happens in each step. Explain why it’s important, what tools you need, and any safety warnings. Highlight the benefits of your process, especially if it offers something better than a competitor’s method.

5. Identify Substeps

  • If there are substeps within a main step, point those out clearly. Make sure they’re placed correctly in the process so readers don’t confuse them with major steps. Avoid putting a substep in the wrong spot.

Style

  • When explaining a process, the way you use words and sentences is really important. Your writing should be clear and easy to understand, just like you would explain something to a friend. It’s also essential to use the right language that fits the information you're sharing.  

  • A process is simply a series of steps that happen over time and have specific causes and effects. To help your readers follow along and remember what you’re saying, it’s useful to include connecting words and phrases. Think of these words as the glue that holds everything together. They guide readers smoothly from one step to the next, making it easier to see how everything fits. For example, you might use words like "first," "next," "then," and "finally" to show the order of events. These little guides help people understand and keep track of where they are in the explanation.

  • When you write sentences in a process explanation, you can choose between active and passive voice. You’ll probably find yourself using the passive voice more often. This is because, most of the time, the focus is on the process itself like scientific principles or how a machine worksrather than on who is doing it. Also, stick to the present tense for your verbs, such as "drives," "alternates," and "interlocks." This helps emphasize that the process is ongoing and relevant, whether it’s happening now, has happened in the past, or will happen in the future. After all, you’re helping someone understand a process that is true across time. By keeping your language simple and clear, using connecting words, and focusing on the process itself, you make it easier for your readers to grasp what you're explaining. This approach not only aids understanding but also makes your writing more engaging.

Visuals

  • Visuals play a crucial role in explaining a process. They help illustrate the order and connections between different steps, making it easier for readers to grasp the information. When creating visuals, it’s essential to tailor them to your audience's needs. For instance, if your readers are not experts, you wouldn’t want to include complicated chemical formulas or detailed diagrams. Instead, a simple flowchart or illustration would be much more effective.
  • There are various types of visuals that can help clarify a process. If you’re explaining how something is made or how it works, a cutaway drawing can provide valuable insight into the inner workings of an object. Showing a picture of a machine in action can also enhance understanding, as it allows readers to see the process in real time.
  • Some of the most common visuals for explaining processes include block diagrams and flowcharts. A flowchart is particularly useful; it consists of labeled boxes connected by arrows that show how a process moves from start to finish. You can arrange flowcharts to be read from top to bottom or from left to right, depending on what makes the most sense for the information you’re presenting. Using visuals not only makes your explanation clearer but also keeps your readers engaged. They can quickly follow along and understand complex ideas without feeling overwhelmed.
  • Remember, the goal of using visuals is to enhance your explanation, so always consider what will be most helpful for your readers. Whether it's a simple flowchart, a cutaway drawing, or an image of a machine, the right visual can make a big difference in how well your audience understands the process.

Process Explanation Checklist

 

1. Break Down the Process: Divide the explanation into three parts: 

  • When explaining a process, it’s beneficial to divide your explanation into three main parts: an introduction, a body, and a conclusion. This structured approach allows readers to easily navigate through the information, making it less overwhelming. The introduction sets the stage, the body provides detailed guidance, and the conclusion wraps everything up, ensuring that the reader understands the entire process.

  • Introduction (What are we talking about?)
  • Body (How do we do it step by step?)
  • Conclusion  (What did we learn?)

Some short processes may not need a conclusion.

2. In the Introduction

  • In the introduction, begin by clearly defining what the process is about. This is your chance to capture the reader's interest and explain why the process is important or useful. For example, if you are explaining how to bake a cake, you might talk about the joy of baking and how it can be a rewarding experience. Next, mention who typically performs this process and outline the tools or equipment that are necessary. Additionally, providing a quick overview of the main steps involved prepares the reader for what they will learn and helps them understand the overall flow of the process.

3. In the Body

  • The body of your explanation is where you break down the process into clear, actionable steps. It’s essential to list each step in the order they should be completed to avoid confusion. Use straightforward and simple language to make your explanation accessible to all readers, regardless of their prior knowledge. Organizing the information with headings, bullet points, or numbered lists can greatly enhance clarity and make it easier for readers to follow along.

4. In the Conclusion

  • When you reach the conclusion, it’s important to summarize the main points of the process. This recap reinforces what the reader has learned and helps solidify their understanding. You might also take this opportunity to evaluate the process, discussing any challenges or tips for success. Additionally, comparing it with other similar processes, can provide further context and help readers see the nuances between different approaches.

5. Use Transitions

  • Throughout the explanation, using transitions is crucial for maintaining a smooth flow of information. Transitions are words or phrases that connect different parts of your writing, making it easier for readers to follow along. Phrases like “next,” “then,” or “after that” guide the reader through the steps, clarifying how one step leads to another. This helps create a logical progression and keeps the reader engaged.

 6. Use Present Tense

  • In terms of writing style, it’s best to use the present tense throughout your explanation. Writing in the present tense creates a sense of proximity and makes the information feel relevant and engaging, as if you’re guiding the reader through the process in real-time. Additionally, consider using the passive voice where it fits naturally.

7. Include Visuals

  • Including visuals such as images, diagrams, or flowcharts can significantly enhance your explanation. Visuals are valuable tools that can clarify complex steps and provide context that words alone might not convey effectively. For example, if you’re explaining how to set up a computer, a diagram showing the connections between different components can make it much clearer for the reader. Additionally, placing these visuals near the relevant text reinforces the information and helps readers connect the written explanation with the visual representation, enhancing their overall understanding of the process.
